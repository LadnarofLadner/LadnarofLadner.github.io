I graduated from the University of Rhode Island in 2002, with a Bachelor of Science in Marine Biology, and, while kicking around my (other) hometown, Warwick, RI, looking for academic jobs--and, more to the point, for a PhD position--I stumbled across an interesting website concerning silicon image sensors. These unique sensors, developed by Federico Faggin, at Foveon Inc., utilized the differntial absorption depth of each wavelength of visible light as a unique way of separating the color visual channels, instead of the standard method of a color filter array, CFA array, or the dichroic mirror-filter splitting prism (i.e. a 3-CCD setup).


This was the beginning, at age 22, of my lifelong study of optical and electrical engineering, a task that really gained momentum, circa 2005, when I purchased James Janesick's book "Scientific Charge-Coupled Devices" [16]. It was also around this time that I became acquainted with Rudolf Luneberg's work on Non-Euclidean visual space [21] (which I somewhat disagreed with), and Polyak's book on the vertebrate retina [3], Helmholtz's classic work, Rudolph Kingslake's work on photographic lens design [7], Oyster's work on the anatomy and function of the human eye [1], and Smith and Atchison's compendium of optical models of the eye [8]. But progress was incremental, and there are far too many specific, minor and major works, inbetween those listed, to proceed in narrative fashion. By about 2010, I had most of the knowledge needed to attempt a geometric study, on a Euclidean basis, of (1) human object space, (2) human image space, and (3) human visual space.

Sometime circa 2004-2005, whilst house-sitting in Calais or Woodbury, VT, I happenend upon the owner's art materials and, having recently heavily perused Eugene Hecht's textbook "Optics", I sat down at their drafting table to create an admittedly limited sketch of the monocular field of view of the human animal. Hecht had noted that, for a young eye, 12.5 cm (about 5 inches) is the near-point of accommodation , and 1 to 3 to 5 meters, depending upon illumination levels and the resulting reflexive diameter of the pupil, was the far-point of accomodation; at which point, the optical system effectively is focussed to infinity, or, rather, has infinite depth of field. As early as 2003 I had stumbled upon the foveon sensor, and the company's website, and I had read a bit about Faggin and the plan to sense colors, at depth, in silicon. I had, even, at this early time, looked a bit into the structure of the human retina and it struck me that the differential resolution with respect to visual angle, or retinal eccentricty, was a far more interesting aspect of the imaging system, than the particular methodology employed to sense color at every pixel, at near 100% fill factor (silicon senses dark and light, only, though, with higher energy photons, multiple hole-electron pairs per photon are generated, according to the equation " Ni = E (eV) / Ee-h, where ηi is the ideal quantum yield (electrons/interacting photon) and Ee-h is the energy required to generate an electron-hole pair, which for silicon is 3.65 eV/e− at room temperature." (Janesick).)
