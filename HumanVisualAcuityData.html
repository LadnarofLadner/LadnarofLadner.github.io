Most of my discussions of human visual acuity, and in particuklar, peripheral visual acuity and the concomitant or requisite peripheral bandwidth for robotic imagers, i.e. foveated imagers, begins with Wertheim's 1894 (1898? 1891?) paper, as translated in the 1980s by Irving Dunsky. I believe the title is simply "Peripheral Visual Acuity". Additionally, Oyster's textbook is an invaluable resource in stating the multiple facets of the problem, and provides very in-depth and precise references to the esssential psychophysical data sets and historical items. I also found Lorrin Riggs disussion of the four different types of acuity, found in C. Graham's work, circa 1960s, to be useful and escpecially so, since, of the four types of acuity, only one is "standard camera" acuity (i.e. dependent primarily upon lens, stops, avialble light levels, and receptor size), while two types, detectuion and recogniton have more to do with "cognition" or, at the very least, "computation", i.e. "computer vision" or image sensor processing. The fourth type of acuity he mentions is vernier acuity, or "super acuity" which, presumably, in the human visual system has something to do with saccades, and with vision processing, too.
