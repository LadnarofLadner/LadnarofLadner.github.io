I was born in Plainfield, VT (Berlin, VT) on July 14th, 1980, Bastille Day, Woody Guthrie's, Matthew Lyon's and, also, Gerald Ford's birthday. Plainfield is about 45 miles north-northwest of Hanover, NH, and 1980 is some 25 years after the summer research conference on A.I. was undertaken, in 1956.

In October of 2003, shortly after recieving my undergraduate degree (Marine Biology, Bachelor of Science, University of Rhode Island, 08/23/2002), I was studying for the Graduate Record Exam (see scores here), and happened upon the section on permutations and combinations. I remembered these equations from high school, because my friends all were in Pre-Calculus and I was catching up in Geometry and Agenbra II, and they all had this one teacher who insisted that the school lockers be referred to as "permutation locks" and not as we colloquially referred to them, as combination locks. His name was Mr. Cashey (spelling?) and he had coke-bottle glasses and a round apple shape, but a pretty tall guy, if I recall correctly. I was reading, at that moment in 2003, a few good books that sent my mind in the direction of A.I. for this next century, and millennium, and, really, spurred me to start a corporation, (Sams Antics Inc., State of Rhode Island, July 2004) to investigate my hypotheses, especially if academia and "the market" was going to shut me out (as, eventually, proved to be the case). Chief on my reading list were, the continued readings in Daniel Dennett's book "Darwin's Dangerous Idea" along with the vast corpus of Stephen Jay Goulds's "Reflections on Natural History" series, as well as his posthumous magnum opus, "The Structure of Evolutionary Theory", but also, in spring of 2003, continued readings in Paul Thompson's "the Structure of Biological Theories", and Elisabeth Lloyd's similarly titled and themed book, as recently discovered by me (my interest in Thompson stretching back to about Spring 2001), "The Structure and Confirmation of Evolutionary Theory". (See my page on "Some Comments on History and Philosophy of Biology", here... link to come). The summer of 2003, as I recall, while still working at the mortgage branch of a local and regional bank, I took out two books from the university library, "Semiconductor Devices", by Sze, and a historical work by Desmond King-Hele, "Erasmus Darwin: A Life of Unparalleled Achievment". I also had purchased, just after graduation, in the summer of 2002, a book by Philip Leiberman, "Human Language and the Reptilian Brain", and Noam Chomsky's latest academic work, "New Horizons in the Study of Language and Mind". All of these works sent my mind moving in particular directions, and, though unappreciated by me at this time, I think Gould's opening essay of his decades long essay series, "Darwin's Delay" had great impact upon me, since, some years earlier, in the thick of my college studies, it probably spurred me to find Darwin's notebooks, particularly his M and N Notebooks, and read them. It is possible that I just stumbled upon the books myself, or in searching a concordance of Darwin's works, for the names "Hume" or "Locke" pinpointed those references? However I found them, I was curious about, and subsequently aware of, Darwin's Post-Beagle opinions about the empiricist philosophers, and may even have been aware of his penchant for the more fashionable, 19th century philosopher August Comte. However it happened, this empirical, somewhat "blank slate" approach to language and mind was intriguing to me, and "materialist" was not exactly a bad word, in my mind, since much scientific progress has been known to occur just by dispensing with metaphysical presumptions, as history has frequently shown. And so, being thus prepared, and with a healthy dose of Chomksyian "Poverty of Stumulus" arguments and "Natural Language", "Generative Grammar", "i-language", or "Transformational Grammar" concepts, the permutation equations suggested, to me, a path forward. I must, at this point, give a shout out to my friend Jenna Knasin, who prepared my mind, in some small degree, by mentioning the six permutations identified by linguistic typologists (in addition to advising me about the Rodnay Zaks 6502 assembly language book (the one with the "Flying Chip" cover) I had inherited from my father's old collection of New England Tech textbooks, and also, about my Barry Brey 8086 and Pentium assembly language textbook; also, advising me regarding high-level languages and their relationship to machine language and assmebly, and, pointing me to the somewhat-dated but still relevant A.I. language, LISP, which I persued at my very next visits to the library and bookstores. I am not certain he used the phrase "lingusitic typology", or even that it was clear in my mind that this was what it was called, but I definitely recall us discussing Subject, Verb and Object, word order. Some five, or so, years later, while perusing a free or discounted book pile in the basement of UVM (University of Vermont), I acquired David Crystal'a "The Cambridge Encyclopedia of Language" (1st or 2nd edition, I think?), with a clear and distinct image of Yoda--of Star Wars film fame--diagraming the six hypothetical word orders to which human languages have a strong tendency to adhere. This clear representation in an academic book, illustrating and confirming my vague discussions with Jenna, and my fortuitious stumbling upon permutation math n my study-session for increased accreditation, was impetus enough, for nthe rest of my life, for me to pursue this idea, doggedly./p>

I was also reading some symbolic logic, and Bertrand Russel at the time (circa, especially, Autumn 2003), and it could have been the general trend of my indulgence in philsophical authors and works, or perhaps, it could have been speicifically Daniel Dennett's penchant for the Socratic method (though he seemed often-prone to rhetorical questions which may or may not desire, or expect, an answer, and may walk you down a cul de sac into undesireable conclcusions?).However it happened, I began to ask the question "What if there is no other way to make a sentence?". When I found the perrumtaiton equation, I knew exactly, that "If human thought was guaranteeed, or almost guauranteed, to take a certain form in order to be considered intelligent, knowing the math underlying that form wouold be a significant discovery." It might have been Chomsky quote? It might have been Quine or Bertrand Russel, or Dawkins, or Dennett, or Lieberman quote; I am uncertain. I just know that exact question, in that exact phrasing, stuck in my head, and it seemed that permutations were profoundly responsible for whatever answer might ultimately be devised. "What if there is no other way to make a (an intelligible) sentence?"./p>

And so, additionally, as I learned how simple and sweet and sleek a design the 6502 actually was, and how many algorithms could be executed if given only a few logical, well-timed circuyit rudimentars, it seemd to me that making, instead of a two pronged, forked ALU, with one output on the bootom, that a three-pronged, hardware designed permutator of word-tokens, would be very much deisreable, for speed and efficientcy of computation. One final reminiscence, on this score, is that I recall Mrs. Thorpe, and my Algebra II class, in Tollgate High School (Warwick Rhode Island). I rememeber we were learning matrix multiplicationa and she assigned the odd problems only, because the answers were in the back of the book, and matrix math was hard. We could check our answers. And they were long problems, so she said we didn't have to write them out. But I did the odd and even problems and wrote the whole things out, becuase I liked them very much (And Start rek Next generation, followed by a half hour sitcom, probably Seinfeld, and then Jeopardy, was about 2 hours long, and i could just nerd out with my favorite TV in the background). It was one of my favorite types of math, and though I later studied lens design, I never did apply that particular method in my attemnops at lens design 9poreferring computer CAD programs, in this modern age). And so, imagine my surprise when I study a little bit about our recent AI bubble, and legitimate boom, and discover that, while the transistors are in fact functioning digitally, they do "reinforce" (or the equivalent) certain pathways, a bit like an analog, wet-chemistry, neural net. Congratualtions! I always said I doubted that neural nets would be of much use, but I also always said that I'd be the first to congratualte them if they did have a breakthrough technology. And so, here I am, officially congratulating them. But it is still not where AI is going; it is just a rest stop. Imagine my surprise though, when I studied the architecture a little (and I mean a LITTLE) closer, and saw that my old friend matrix multiplication--and even markov models, hidden markov models and other probability concepts--were all wrapped up in this new and hot technology !!! I felt 20 years aheaed of the game !!! Though, I guess, I always feel about twenty years ahead of the game (see my page on olfactory research, here, when I get it written and proofread)./p>
