I was born in Plainfield, VT (Berlin, VT) on July 14th, 1980, Bastille Day, Woody Guthrie's, Matthew Lyon's and, also, Gerald Ford's birthday. Plainfield is about 45 miles north-northwest of Hanover, NH, and 1980 is some 25 years after the summer research conference on A.I. was undertaken, in 1956.

In October of 2003, shortly after recieving my undergraduate degree (Marine Biology, Bachelor of Science, University of Rhode Island, 08/23/2002), I was studying for the Graduate Record Exam (see scores here), and happened upon the section on permutations and combinations. I remembered these equations from high school, because my friends all were in Pre-Calculus and I was catching up in Geometry and Agenbra II, and they all had this one teacher who insisted that the school lockers be referred to as "permutation locks" and not as we colloquially referred to them, as combination locks. His name was Mr. Cashey (spelling?) and he had coke-bottle glasses and a round apple shape, but a pretty tall guy, if I recall correctly. I was reading, at that moment in 2003, a few good books that sent my mind in the direction of A.I. for this next century, and millennium, and, really, spurred me to start a corporation, (Sams Antics Inc., State of Rhode Island, July 2004) to investigate my hypotheses, especially if academia and "the market" was going to shut me out (as, eventually, proved to be the case). Chief on my reading list were, the continued readings in Daniel Dennett's book "Darwin's Dangerous Idea" along with the vast corpus of Stephen Jay Goulds's "Reflections on Natural History" series, as well as his posthumous magnum opus, "The Structure of Evolutionary Theory", but also, in spring of 2003, continued readings in Paul Thompson's "the Structure of Biological Theories", and Elisabeth Lloyd's similarly titled and themed book, as recently discovered by me (my interest in Thompson stretching back to about Spring 2001), "The Structure and Confirmation of Evolutionary Theory". (See my page on "Some Comments on History and Philosophy of Biology", here... link to come). The summer of 2003, as I recall, while still working at the mortgage branhc of a local and regional bank, I took out two books from the Univeristy library, "Semiconductor Devices", by Sze, and a historical work by Desmond King-Hele, "Erasmus Darwin: A Life of Unparalleled Achievment". I also had purchased, just after graduation, in the summer of 2002, a book by Philip Leiberman, "Human Language and the Reptilian Brain", and Noam Chomsky's latest academic work, "New Horizons in the Study of Language and Mind".
